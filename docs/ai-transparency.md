# How Throughline Uses AI, and Why You Can Trust the Evaluation

This page explains, in plain terms, how the Throughline evaluation tool works,
what part technology plays in it, and what part it does not. It is written for
two audiences: the participants whose growth is being evaluated, and the
organizations deciding whether to send their people to the workshop. Evaluation
shapes people's futures, so you deserve to know exactly how yours is put
together.

## The short version

Throughline does not judge anyone. People do. The tool collects what real human
evaluators observed, organizes it against a rubric that real humans wrote and
approved, and drafts a summary that a real human reads, corrects, and signs off
on before it ever reaches you. At every step the evidence stays visible and
traceable back to the moment it was observed. The technology makes human
judgment more careful and more personal, not less human.

## What the tool does, and what it does not

The AI in Throughline has one narrow job: to take the free-form notes that
facilitators write while watching an activity and turn them into tidy, organized
observations, each tied to a specific skill and a specific piece of evidence. It
is a clerk, not a judge.

It does not decide your score on its own. It does not invent evidence. It does
not send anything to you or to your organization without a human reading it
first. It does not compare you to other people behind the scenes or rank you by
some hidden formula. Everything it produces is a draft that a facilitator can
confirm, adjust, or reject.

Put simply: the AI never has the last word, and it never has the only word.

## How your evaluation is actually built

Every rating you receive can be traced backward through a clear chain. This is
the heart of how Throughline works.

First, a facilitator watches you in a real activity and writes down what they
saw, in their own words, at the time they saw it. That note is timestamped and
kept.

Second, the tool organizes that note into specific observations, each attached
to one of the workshop's skill areas and to the rubric that the workshop leaders
wrote and agreed on ahead of time. The rubric is not secret; it describes what
each level of competence looks like.

Third, where more than one facilitator observed you, the tool holds their
observations side by side. If they agree, that is noted. If they disagree, the
tool flags it and a human works out the difference before anything is finalized.
Nothing gets quietly averaged away.

Fourth, a human evaluator reviews the draft, checks it against the evidence,
corrects anything that is off, and only then approves it. The final report you
receive has been read and ratified by a person who is accountable for it.

Because each step is recorded, you can always ask, "Where did this come from?"
and get a real answer: this facilitator, in this activity, saw this, and read it
against this part of the rubric. If you want to discuss it, or push back on how
something was interpreted, there is a concrete record to talk about rather than a
vague impression.

## Why we built it this way

Most evaluation is either thin or opaque. A busy workshop makes it easy for real
observations to be forgotten, for feedback to arrive too late to act on, and for
a final judgment to rest on whoever happened to remember the most at the end.
Throughline was built to fix that, and the benefits all point the same
direction: toward more human, more useful evaluation.

It is personal. Because every observation is captured as it happens and tied to
you specifically, your evaluation reflects what you actually did, not a general
impression.

It is traceable. Every rating has its evidence attached. You are never left
guessing why you received a given score.

It is timely. The tool helps facilitators see, day by day, who needs more
intentional attention and where a follow-up conversation would help. That means
feedback and support reach you while there is still time to grow, not months
later.

It is honest about disagreement. When two evaluators see you differently, that is
surfaced and worked out by people, not hidden.

It keeps humans in charge. The technology does the tedious work of collecting and
organizing so that the people evaluating you can spend their attention on
judgment, encouragement, and genuine conversation.

## How we test and vet the tool

Trusting the tool should not require taking our word for it, so we hold it to a
standard before it is used on anyone's real evaluation.

We check that the tool's organized observations faithfully match what the
facilitator actually wrote, with no additions or distortions. We check that
scores never move without the evidence to support them and a human to confirm
them. We check that disagreements between evaluators are always flagged rather
than smoothed over. And we confirm that no report reaches a participant or an
organization until a human has reviewed and approved it.

## For participants

Your evaluation is about helping you grow, and it belongs, in an important sense,
to you. You can see the evidence behind it. You can ask where a rating came from.
If a conversation is recommended because something needs attention, that is not a
verdict against you; it is the workshop investing in you, and how you respond to
that feedback is itself something we value and record with care.

## For organizations considering the workshop

When you send someone to this workshop, you are trusting us with a decision that
affects their path. Throughline is how we honor that trust. It does not replace
human evaluators with a machine. It gives your people evaluations that are more
individualized, better evidenced, and more accountable than what unaided memory
and end-of-week impressions can produce, with a clear record you are welcome to
review.

## The pros, at a glance

- Evidence-based: every rating traces to a specific, timestamped observation.
- Traceable: participants and mentors can always walk back from a score to why.
- Human-ratified: no AI output is final; a person checks and approves every report.
- Rubric-anchored: judged against criteria humans wrote and approved in advance.
- Personal, not anonymous: higher context and more individual attention, not less.
- Timely: surfaces who needs support while there is still time to act.
- Honest about conflict: evaluator disagreements are flagged, not averaged away.
- A tight feedback loop: growth areas trigger real, recorded follow-up conversations.
